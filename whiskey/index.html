Whiskey Behavior

whis key

/ˈ(h)wiskē/

Noun

1. A spirit distilled from malted grain, esp. barley or rye.
2. A code word representing the letter W, used in radio communication

Whiskey is a distilled spirit obtained from the fermented mash of grain, distilled at less than 190 proof, stored in oak containers and bottled at a minimum of 40% abv. Gin and vodka are also distilled from a mash of grain, but whiskey's maturation process in oak containers is what makes it unique. Oak is air permeable, which helps oxidize the distillate and releases flavor compounds through wood chemicals such as lignin and vanillin.

Whiskey is also initially distilled at a low alcohol by volume, which allows flavor compounds called congeners remain in the distillate. Therefore, the flavor of whiskey is not compromised, and retains a fuller, richer taste. Whiskey is ultimately an umbrella term: just as beer has a variety of styles, whiskey also has a number of varieties that fall under its general description.
