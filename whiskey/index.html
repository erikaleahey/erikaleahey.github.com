whis · key

/′(h)wiskē/

Noun

1. A spirit distilled from malted grain, esp. barley or rye.
2. A code word representing the letter W, used in radio communication

Whiskey is a distilled spirit obtained from the fermented mash of grain, distilled at less than 190 proof, stored in oak containers and bottled at a minimum of 40% abv. Gin and vodka are also distilled from a mash of grain, but whiskey's maturation process in oak containers is what makes it unique. Oak is air permeable, which helps oxidize the distillate and releases flavor compounds through wood chemicals such as lignin and vanillin.

Whiskey is also initially distilled at a low alcohol by volume, which allows flavor compounds called congeners remain in the distillate. Therefore, the flavor of whiskey is not compromised, and retains a fuller, richer taste. Whiskey is ultimately an umbrella term: just as beer has a variety of styles, whiskey also has a number of varieties that fall under its general description.

Truckloads of grain are shipped directly from farms to the whiskey manufacturer to be stored in silos. It is inspected and cleaned to remove dust and foreign particles. All grains except barley are ground into a meal in a gristmill. The meal is mixed with water and cooked to break down the cellulose walls that starch granules.

Instead of being cooked, the barley is malted. This is achieved by soaking the barley in water until it is thoroughly saturated. Then it is spread out and sprinkled with water for about three weeks, until it begins to sprout. During this germination, the enzyme amylase is produced, which converts the starch in the barley to sugars. To halt the sprouting, barley is dried and heated with hot air from a kiln. The malted barley is then ground like other grains.

Mashing consists of mixing the cooked grain with the malted barley and warm water. The amylase in the malted barley converts the starch in the other grains to sugars. After several hours, the mixture is converted into a sugar-rich liquid known as mash.

The mash is transferred to a fermentation vessel, usually open in the United States. These vessels can be made out of wood, or stainless steel (for bourbon, always wood). Yeast is added to begin fermentation, which converts the sugars in the mash to alcohol. The yeast can be added as new yeast cells (sweet mash process) or by using a portion of a previous batch of fermentation (sour mash).

The sour mash process is often used because it's effective at room temperature, and its low pH promotes yeast growth and inhibits the growth of bacteria. The sweet mash process is more difficult to control, and must be used at temperatures above 80°F. After three or four days, the end product, called "distillers beer"is a liquid containing about 10% alcohol.

Most modern distilleries use a continuous still to distill their wash. A continuous still consists of a tall cylindrical column filled with perforated plates. Steam enters from the bottom, and the distiller's beer enters from the top.

The beer is distilled as it slowly drips through the plates, and the alcohol is condensed back into liquid. The product is called "low wine", which is then distilled a second time to produce "high wine", or whiskey that contains about 70% alcohol.

Water is added to the high wine to reduce it's alcohol content to about 50-60%. American whiskeys are aged in warm, dry conditions so that they lose water and become more alcoholic. Whiskey is aged in wooden barrels, usually made of charred white oak. White oak is one of the few woods than can hold liquid without leaking, but allows for the whiskey to move within the pores of the wood, which helps add flavor. The barrels are usually new and only used once.

There are three factors to the aging process. First, the mixture of water, alcohol, and congeners react with each other over time. Then, the ingredients react with oxygen in oxidation reactions. Third, the water absorbs substances from the wood as it moves within it. Whiskey generally takes three or four years to mature, with many being aged ten or fifteen years.

Straight and single malt whiskeys are not blended: they're produced from single batches and bottled straight from the barrel. For the rest, different batches of whiskey are mixed together to produce a better flavor. Neutral grain flavor is often added to lighten the flavor, caramel is added to standardize the color, and small amounts of sherry or port wine are added to blend flavors.
